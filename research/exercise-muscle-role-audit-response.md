# OpenEvidence Response — EMG Verification of Rehabilitation Exercise Muscle Roles

Retrieved: 2026-03-29

---

## GROUP 1: LOWER EXTREMITY

### Bridge
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Gluteus maximus: 20-40% MVIC in standard bridge [1-3]
- Hamstrings (biceps femoris, semitendinosus, semimembranosus): 15-25% MVIC [1][4]
- Gluteus medius: Active as stabilizer, specific values vary by hip position [2][5]
- Erector spinae: 10-20% MVIC [1][4-5]
- Transversus abdominis/multifidus: Moderate activation as core stabilizers [2][4]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Gluteus maximus (though often <40% MVIC in standard bridge)
- Secondary movers: Hamstrings (20-25% MVIC)
- Stabilizers: Gluteus medius, erector spinae, multifidus, transversus abdominis

**Key Findings:**
- Hirose & Tsuruike (2018): Gluteus maximus activity remained consistent across different knee angles, while hamstring activity varied with knee position [1]
- Choi et al. (2015): Adding isometric hip abduction with Thera-Band significantly increased gluteus maximus activity while maintaining similar hamstring and erector spinae levels [6]
- Ekstrom et al. (2007): Standard bridge produced EMG levels <45% MVIC, suggesting more appropriate for endurance/stabilization training [3]
- Foot position and knee angle do not dramatically change the dominant mover; single-leg variations significantly increase activation levels [2-3]

### Clamshell
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Gluteus medius: 26-67% MVIC depending on position and study [7-10]
- TFL: 20-34% MVIC [7][9-11]
- Gluteus maximus: 25-34% MVIC [8-9]
- Piriformis: 14.5% T2 change (fMRI study) [12]
- Gluteus minimus: 4.5-7.2% T2 change [12]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Gluteus medius (consistently >40% MVIC in optimal positions)
- Secondary movers: Gluteus maximus, TFL (both typically 20-35% MVIC)
- Contributors: Piriformis, gluteus minimus, deep external rotators

**Hip Flexion Angle Effects:**
- Willcox & Burden (2013): Gluteus medius activation greatest at 60° hip flexion compared to 30° or 45° [7]
- Sidorkewicz et al. (2014): Varying hip flexion angle did not significantly affect gluteus medius-to-TFL ratio [11]

**Gluteus Medius-to-TFL Ratio:**
- Selkowitz et al. (2013) fine-wire EMG: Clamshell produced highest gluteal-to-TFL activation index (115) [10]

**Commonly Overlooked:**
- Kim et al. (2025) fMRI: Significant deep external rotator activation — obturator internus (24.2% T2), gemellus superior (18.7%), piriformis (14.5%) [12]

### Squat (Bodyweight)
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Vastus lateralis/medialis: 40-70% MVIC (highest activation) [13-15]
- Rectus femoris: 18-35% MVIC [16]
- Gluteus maximus: 16.9-35.4% MVIC (increases with depth) [13][17-18]
- Biceps femoris (hamstrings): 15-25% MVIC [13-15]
- Gluteus medius: 30-45% MVIC [13]
- Erector spinae: 13-16% MVIC [15]

**Depth Effects:**
- Caterisano et al. (2002): Gluteus maximus relative contribution increased with depth — partial (16.9%), parallel (28.0%), full (35.4%) [18]
- Gorsuch et al. (2013): Rectus femoris and erector spinae significantly higher during parallel versus partial squats [15]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Quadriceps (vastus lateralis/medialis consistently >40% MVIC)
- Secondary movers: Gluteus maximus (becomes primary at full depth >40% MVIC), rectus femoris (20-35%)
- Stabilizers/Co-contractors: Hamstrings (co-contraction pattern, 15-25% MVIC), erector spinae, core musculature
- McCurdy et al. (2018): Gluteus maximus EMG significantly greater than hamstring EMG during bilateral squats (40.3% vs 24.4%) [17]

### Sit-to-Stand
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Quadriceps (vastus medialis): 30-50% MVIC [19]
- Biceps femoris: 15-25% MVIC [19]
- Tibialis anterior: Significantly active during forward-lean phase [20-21]
- Erector spinae: 20-35% MVIC [19][21]
- Rectus abdominis: 15-25% MVIC [19]

**Differences from Squat:**
- Goulart & Valls-Solé (1999): Tibialis anterior, abdominals, and SCM activated first as preparatory postural muscles; lumbar paraspinals, quadriceps, and hamstrings form executional pattern time-locked to seat-off [20]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Quadriceps, lumbar paraspinals (executional muscles)
- Preparatory/Stabilizers: Tibialis anterior (forward lean), abdominals, hamstrings

### Heel Raise (Standing Calf Raise)
**EMG Activation Levels (Knee Straight):**
- Gastrocnemius medialis: ~23% MVIC [22]
- Gastrocnemius lateralis: ~23% MVIC [22]
- Soleus: ~23% MVIC [22]

**EMG Activation Levels (Knee Bent 45°):**
- Gastrocnemius medialis: ~18% MVIC (5% decrease) [22]
- Gastrocnemius lateralis: ~18% MVIC (5% decrease) [22]
- Soleus: ~27% MVIC (4% increase) [22]

**Key Findings:**
- Hébert-Losier et al. (2012): 4-5% change might not be clinically significant enough to substantially influence muscle-specific benefits [22]
- Hali et al. (2019): Medial and lateral gastrocnemius motor unit recruitment thresholds higher and firing rates lower when knee flexed; soleus motor unit properties not affected by knee position [23]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers (knee straight): Gastrocnemius and soleus co-primary (~23% MVIC each)
- Primary movers (knee bent): Soleus becomes relatively more dominant (27% vs 18%)

### Straight Leg Raise
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Psoas major: Peak at end-range hip flexion, >60% MVIC in late concentric phase [24-25]
- Rectus femoris: ~40-50% MVIC [24][26]
- TrA/Internal oblique: Significantly active in mid-to-late concentric and hold phases [24][27]

**Key Findings:**
- Okubo et al. (2021) fine-wire EMG: Psoas major EMG significantly higher than rectus femoris during late concentric, hold, and early eccentric phases [24]
- Hu et al. (2011): Psoas active both ipsilaterally and contralaterally during ASLR — functions primarily as lumbar spine stabilizer [28]
- Juan et al. (2024): >60% MVIC iliopsoas activity during ASLR at ~60° hip flexion [25]
- Beales et al. (2009): Greater ipsilateral obliquus internus and externus activation [27]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Psoas major (especially at end-range >60°), rectus femoris (co-primary but lower activation)
- Stabilizers: TrA/internal oblique (core stabilization, especially mid-to-late range) [24][27]
- Contributors: Tensor fasciae latae, adductor longus [28]

### Sidelying Hip Adduction
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Adductor longus: 14-108% MVIC depending on exercise variation [29]
- Adductor magnus: Proximal and distal portions show different activation patterns [30-31]

**Key Findings:**
- Serner et al. (2014): Sidelying hip adduction with elastic band — 14-48% nEMG; Copenhagen adduction exercise — 108% nEMG [29]
- Kato et al. (2019): Adductor longus more active at hip-extended positions; adductor magnus recruited regardless of hip position [30]
- Benn et al. (2018) fine-wire EMG: Adductor magnus has functionally distinct proximal and distal portions [31]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Adductor longus, adductor magnus (both can achieve >40% MVIC in appropriate exercises)
- Secondary movers: Gracilis, adductor brevis
- Core activation: Moderate during sidelying position for stability

### Seated Hip Internal Rotation
**Primary Hip Internal Rotators (Controversial):**
- Gluteus medius (anterior fibers): Active during IR tasks [32-33]
- Gluteus minimus: 18.1% T2 change during hip ABD with ER (fMRI) [12]
- TFL: Martins et al. (2022) — myoelectric activity increased ~90% at 90° hip flexion with increasing medial rotation torque, but no systematic increase at 0° or 45° [34]
- Adductor magnus: Distal portion active during IR [31]

**Key Controversy:**
- Martins et al. (2022): TFL may lack a moment arm for medial rotation in extended positions; gluteus maximus superior portion showed increased activity at 0° hip extension with medial rotation torque [34]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Context-dependent — gluteus medius/minimus (especially flexed positions), TFL (primarily at 90° flexion), adductor magnus distal portion
- Hip flexion angle dramatically affects which muscles serve as primary internal rotators

## GROUP 2: UPPER EXTREMITY & SHOULDER

### Overhead Press (Seated)
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Anterior deltoid: 60-90% MVIC [35-37]
- Middle deltoid: 50-80% MVIC [35-37]
- Posterior deltoid: 20-40% MVIC [36-37]
- Upper trapezius: 30-50% MVIC [35-36]
- Triceps brachii: 40-60% MVIC [36-37]
- Pectoralis major: 30-50% MVIC (higher in front press) [35]
- Serratus anterior: 20-40% MVIC [38-39]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Anterior deltoid (>60%), middle deltoid (>50%)
- Secondary movers: Triceps brachii (40-60%), upper trapezius (30-50%), pectoralis major (30-50%)
- Stabilizers: Serratus anterior, infraspinatus (GH stabilizer)
- Synergist: Supraspinatus (assists abduction component)

**Key Findings:**
- Paoli et al. (2010): Widest ROM (180° elbow extension) increased EMG activity of all muscles except middle trapezius and teres minor [36]
- Williams et al. (2020): Unstable conditions significantly increased serratus anterior, rhomboids, and latissimus dorsi activation [39]

### Resisted Internal Rotation
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Subscapularis (upper and lower): 44-81.7% MVIC depending on position [41-43]
- Pectoralis major: Significantly more active with arm in front of body [41][44]

**Position Effects:**
- Suenaga et al. (2003): Subscapularis highest (81.7% MMT) during resistive IR at zero-position; only 2-12% MMT during IR with arm at side or at 90° abduction [43]
- Greis et al. (1996): Subscapularis significantly more active (~70% MVC) during lift-off test; pectoralis major more active during resisted IR with arm in front [41]
- Ginn et al. (2017): Belly press test was the only task where subscapularis was significantly higher than all other internal rotators [42]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Subscapularis (especially lift-off and zero-position), pectoralis major (arm in front)
- Secondary movers: Latissimus dorsi, teres major, anterior deltoid

### Wall Push-Up
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Serratus anterior: 27.55% RVC (wall push-up plus) [46]
- Pectoralis major: 30-50% MVIC [46-47]
- Triceps brachii: ~30-40% MVIC [47-48]
- Rectus abdominis: 10-20% MVIC [47]
- External oblique: 10-20% MVIC [47]

**Key Findings:**
- Park et al. (2014): Wall push-up plus — 27.55% RVC vs knee push-up plus 39.48% vs standard push-up plus 77.09% for serratus anterior [46]
- Herrington et al. (2015): Serratus anterior showed greatest activation across all press-up exercises; plus component essential [50]
- Marcolin et al. (2015): Wall push-ups with reduced load show minimal core activation (10-20% MVIC) [47]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Pectoralis major (30-50%), triceps (30-40%)
- Stabilizers: Serratus anterior (meaningful only with plus component), core muscles (minimal in wall variation)

### Bicep Curl (Standard)
**Grip Position Effects (Established Biomechanics):**
- Supinated grip: Biceps brachii primary, brachialis co-primary, brachioradialis secondary
- Neutral (hammer) grip: Brachialis and brachioradialis co-primary, biceps reduced
- Pronated grip: Brachialis primary, brachioradialis co-primary, biceps significantly reduced
- Supinator: Active during supinated curl to maintain supination, not a primary elbow flexor

### Tricep Extension (Overhead)
**Key Findings:**
- Paoli et al. (2010) and Saeterbakken & Fimland (2013): Triceps activation 40-60% MVIC during overhead press, heads not differentiated [36-37]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary mover: Long head (preferentially recruited in overhead position due to length-tension relationship)
- Co-primary movers: Lateral and medial heads
- Synergist: Anconeus
- Stabilizers: Shoulder stabilizers (rotator cuff, deltoid)

### Scapular Retraction Exercise
**Key Findings:**
- Cools et al. (2020): Middle trapezius activation ranged from <10% to >20% MVIC depending on exercise progression [40]
- Williams et al. (2020): Rhomboid activation increased during unstable overhead press [39]
- Lower trapezius often lower than middle trapezius during retraction-focused exercises [39-40]
- Levator scapulae: Low-to-moderate activation during retraction (primarily elevates)

### Resisted External Rotation
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Gaudet et al. (2018) isokinetic ER: Infraspinatus high peak activity (+36.5% MVA); supraspinatus moderate to high; middle and posterior deltoids higher peak activity [45]
- Lang et al. (2021): External rotator muscle activity increased with higher elevation angles [52]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Infraspinatus, teres minor
- Stabilizers: Supraspinatus (GH stabilization)
- Position does not dramatically change primary activation pattern

## GROUP 3: WRIST, HAND & FOREARM

### Forearm Pronation/Supination
**Based on Anatomical/Biomechanical Principles:**
- Pronation: Pronator teres (primary, power), pronator quadratus (co-primary, fine control)
- Supination: Biceps brachii (primary when elbow flexed + resistance high), supinator (primary all positions, especially elbow extended)
- Brachioradialis: Contributes in neutral zone

### Wrist Radial and Ulnar Deviation
- Radial deviation: FCR and ECRL co-primary (synergistic action cancels flexion/extension)
- Ulnar deviation: FCU and ECU co-primary (same synergistic mechanism)
- Wrist and finger extensors/flexors provide dynamic stability

### Grip Strengthening
- FDS: High activation during grip
- FDP: High activation during grip
- FPL: Active during thumb flexion component
- Extensor digitorum: Functions as **stabilizer** (maintains wrist extension during grip)
- Lumbricals and interossei: Moderate activation for finger positioning and MCP flexion

### Thumb Opposition
- Opponens pollicis: Primary mover
- FPB: Co-primary (flexion component)
- APB: Co-primary (abduction component)
- Opponens digiti minimi: Contributes on receiving side
- Opposition is primarily intrinsic muscle function; extrinsic (FPL) provides supplementary flexion

## GROUP 4: SPINE & CORE

### Bird Dog Exercise
**EMG Activation Levels:**
- Gluteus maximus: ~40-56% MVIC (quadruped arm/leg lift) [3]
- Erector spinae: <45% MVIC [3]
- Khosrokiani et al. (2021): Lowest local-to-global muscle ratio (L/G = 1.21) [2]

**Muscle Roles:**
- Primary movers: Gluteus maximus (ipsilateral leg), deltoid (contralateral arm)
- Stabilizers: Multifidus (segmental stabilization), erector spinae, contralateral obliques (anti-rotation)
- Ekstrom et al. (2007): Gluteus maximus >45% MVIC (strengthening potential); other muscles <45% (endurance/stabilization) [3]

### Modified Curl-Up / Dead Bug
**Key Findings:**
- Juker et al. (1998): Psoas activation <10% MVC during curl-ups; significantly lower than sit-ups (15-35% MVC) [53]
- Beales et al. (2009): Greater ipsilateral obliquus internus and externus activation during ASLR-like positions [27]
- Marcolin et al. (2015): Obliquus externus 10-30% MVIC during various exercises [47]
- Multifidus should be considered co-stabilizer [2][4]

### Pelvic Tilt Exercises
- Posterior pelvic tilt: Rectus abdominis primary mover (mechanical advantage)
- Anterior pelvic tilt: Erector spinae and hip flexors co-primary
- Not co-primary across all three groups; specific muscles dominate specific tilt directions

### Diaphragmatic Breathing Exercises
- Beales et al. (2009): Respiratory fluctuation of IAP did not differ during ASLR; breathing patterns maintained during core activation [27]
- External oblique contributes during forced expiration; minimal during quiet diaphragmatic breathing
- Pelvic floor: Limited EMG data; coordination between breathing and pelvic floor suggested [27]

---

## METHODOLOGICAL CONSIDERATIONS
- EMG normalization varies (MVIC, MVC, MMT) — direct cross-study comparisons challenging
- Significant inter-individual variability in muscle activation patterns
- Surface vs fine-wire EMG: Deep muscles require fine-wire for accurate measurement
- >40% MVIC threshold for "primary mover" is somewhat arbitrary; functional context matters
