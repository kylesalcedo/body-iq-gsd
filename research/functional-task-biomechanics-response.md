# OpenEvidence Response — Functional Task Biomechanics

Retrieved: 2026-03-29

---

## Task 1: Carrying and Lifting Objects

### Required Joint Movements and ROM Thresholds
- Bilateral carrying at waist height requires minimal shoulder elevation (typically <30°); one-sided carrying demands greater trunk stabilization and contralateral trunk lean [1-2]
- Upper extremity ROM: 0-121° elbow flexion, -40° to 38° wrist flexion-extension, -28° to 38° wrist ulnar-radial deviation [2]

### Primary Muscles and Activation
- Load mass has the most substantial effect on L5-S1 compression forces [3]
- Asymmetric lifting significantly increases mediolateral shear forces; horizontal load location primarily affects knee and shoulder forces [3]
- Trunk stabilization requires sustained erector spinae and core musculature activation

### Strength Thresholds
- NIOSH lifting equation: load mass, asymmetry angle, horizontal location, and deposit height all significantly affect joint loading [3]
- Increased deposit height → higher shoulder forces; horizontal location affects knees and shoulders most [3]

### Grip Strength Requirements
- Handgrip strength strongly related to physical function and ADL performance [4]
- Every 5-kg decrease in handgrip strength associated with increased ADL limitation odds: 20% for eating, 14% for walking and bathing, 9% for dressing [5]
- Peak mean grip strength: 51 kg males (ages 29-39), 31 kg females (ages 26-42) [6]

### Clinical Significance
- Handgrip strength predicts disability, morbidity, and mortality in older adults [6-7]
- Weak grip (≥2.5 SD below peak mean) reaches 23% prevalence in males, 27% in females by age 80 [6]
- Carrying tasks specifically mentioned as fatigability measures in ADL assessments [4]

### Standardized Assessment Tools
- Handgrip dynamometry correlates well with upper- and lower-body 1RM testing [4]
- AHA recommends grip strength as part of functional capacity evaluation [4]

## Task 2: Dressing — Upper Body

### Required Shoulder ROM
- Overhead shirt donning: 121° ± 6.7° shoulder flexion and 128° ± 7.9° abduction [8]
- Hand-behind-back (bra fastening, tucking): 85° ± 24° glenohumeral internal rotation [9]
- Functional ROM across all ADL: -65°/0°/105° humeral plane angle, 0-108° humeral elevation, -55°/0°/79° humeral rotation [2]

### Hand and Finger Dexterity
- Button manipulation and zipping require fine motor control
- Functional ROM: 0-121° elbow flexion, -40° to 38° wrist flexion-extension [2]

### Critical Muscles for Hand-Behind-Back
- Complex coordinated motion requiring shoulder extension, abduction, and internal rotation with scapular anterior tilt [10]
- Almost all participants exhibit elbow flexion; scapular upward/downward rotation varies between individuals [10]
- Glenohumeral abduction and scapulothoracic upward rotation necessary for most upper body dressing [9]

### Post-Surgical ROM Deficits
- Patients reaching above belt line or thoracic spine have significantly improved functional outcomes [11]
- Hand-behind-back reach serves as clinical marker of global ROM and functional outcomes for shoulder pain [11]

### Clinical Populations
- Post-shoulder arthroplasty [8][12], stroke patients with UE impairment [13]

## Task 3: Dressing — Lower Body

### Hip and Knee Flexion ROM Required
- Tying shoes: 126.1° maximum hip flexion [14]
- Sitting to standing: 103.0° hip flexion [14]
- Getting on/off toilet: 112.6° hip flexion [14]
- Post-THA optimal satisfaction cutoff: 110° flexion and 35-40° external rotation [15]

### Trunk Flexion and Balance Demands
- Standing sock donning requires significant trunk flexion and single-leg balance
- Older adults demonstrate higher trunk flexion and greater postural sway vs younger adults [16]

### Critical Muscles
- Hip flexors, core stabilizers, hand intrinsics for lace manipulation [13]
- Stroke cut-off values for independent dressing: 4 pts hip flexion, 4 pts knee extension, 3 pts abdominal strength [13]

### Compensatory Strategies Post-THA
- Adaptive equipment (sock aids, long-handled shoehorns) commonly used [17]
- 50% of patients stop using prescribed adaptive equipment by 6 weeks post-op [18]
- Modified precautions focusing on avoiding combined flexion + adduction + IR allow faster return [19]

### Minimum Thresholds
- 110° hip flexion critical for independent lower body dressing post-THA [15]
- 35-40° ER during intraoperative stability testing predicts postoperative satisfaction [15]

## Task 4: Bathing and Hygiene

### Shoulder ROM Requirements
- Washing hair: 121° shoulder flexion [8]
- Washing back: 85° ± 24° glenohumeral IR combined with extension [9]
- Washing contralateral axilla: 46° ± 12° humerothoracic elevation [9]

### Balance Demands During Showering
- Greater instability in older adults vs younger adults during standing shower transfers [20]
- Vertical grab bars and wall touch result in safest transfers [20]
- Bath mats improve balance parallel to bathtub rim but limited perpendicular benefit [20]

### Grip Strength for Hygiene Tasks
- Washcloth wringing and faucet turning require functional grip strength
- Males: 51 kg peak, females: 31 kg peak normative values [6]

### Sit-to-Stand for Toilet Transfers
- Lower seat height increases strength demands
- Older adults show slower sit-to-stand time, higher trunk flexion, lower ankle dorsiflexion torque [16]
- Minimum relative muscle power: 1.1 W·kg⁻¹ men, 1.0 W·kg⁻¹ women for five sit-to-stand reps [21]

### First ADL Components Lost
- Bathing among first ADL limitations with decreased handgrip strength (14% increased odds per 5-kg decrease) [5]
- Bathing disability associated with 47% higher hazard for mortality [5]

### Clinical Populations
- SCI patients require specialized shower equipment including rolling commode chairs [22]
- Stroke patients commonly lose bathing independence early

### Standardized Assessment Tools
- FIM includes bathing as scored item; Berg Balance Scale assesses transfer safety [4]

## Task 5: Floor Transfers

### Joint ROM Requirements
- "Sit" technique (most common, 60% of older adults) requires highest hip and knee flexion [23]
- Better knee flexibility (left p=0.019, right p=0.025) and hip flexibility (r=0.514 correlation) predict performance [24]

### Muscle Strength Demands
- Leg extension power significantly correlated with floor rise performance (r=0.725 for time, r=0.582 for components) [24]
- Older adults show significantly lower leg extension power (left p=0.002, right p=0.013) [24]
- Quadriceps, gluteals, and upper extremity push strength all critical

### Sitting-Rising Test as Mortality Predictor
- Significant predictor of all-cause, natural, and cardiovascular mortality [25-26]
- Multivariate-adjusted HR: 3.84 for natural mortality, 6.05 for CV mortality (highest vs lowest SRT groups) [25]
- Each unit increase in SRT score confers 21% improvement in survival [26]
- Scored 0-10; points subtracted for each hand/knee support and unsteadiness [26]

### Movement Strategy Patterns
- Seven components: lying, initiation, positioning, supporting, elevation, stabilization, standing, walking [24]
- Three main techniques: sit-up (60%), side-sit (20%), roll over (20%) [23]
- Median time to rise significantly longer in older subjects (5.7s vs 3.7s, p<0.001) [24]

### Minimum Strength Thresholds
- Resistance training shows trend toward improvement (MD: -0.81[-1.72, 0.09]) [27]
- 90.6% of community-dwelling older adults use initial roll with asymmetrical squat sequence [28]

### Standardized Assessment Tools
- SRT validated [4][25-26]
- Timed supine-to-stand correlates with gait speed (r=-0.61), grip strength (r=-0.30), TUG (r=0.71) [28]

## Task 6: Running and Jogging

### Ankle-Knee-Hip Kinetic Chain
- Stance phase decreases with speed: 62% walking, 31% running, 22% sprinting [29]
- Ankle plantar flexors are major energy generator and absorber (>60%) at all speeds during stance [30]
- Their contribution to whole stride energy generation declines with speed as hip extensors become more dominant [30]

### Peak Muscle Forces During Stance
- Soleus and gastrocnemius lateralis activation during mid-stance shows large positive correlations with leg stiffness (r=0.506-0.552) [31]
- Posterior calf musculature active in late swing through first 80% of stance during running (vs 15% in walking) [29]
- Quadriceps and hamstring activity increases with running speed [29]

### Required Ankle Dorsiflexion
- Anterior compartment muscles undergo concentric contraction at initial contact during running (vs eccentric in walking) [29]

### Hip Extension and Abductor Demands
- Hip extensor positive work rises superlinearly with speed: 3-fold during stance, 12-fold during swing [30]
- Becomes biggest energy generator across whole stride at speeds >5 m·s⁻¹ [30]
- Plantar flexor peak moment accounts for 96% of variance in step length; swing hip extension accounts for 99% of variance in step frequency [30]

### Common Weakness Patterns
- Vastus lateralis and tibialis anterior show detectable activity changes at steepest grades and fastest speeds [32]
- Weak hip abductors → knee valgus; weak calf complex → Achilles tendinopathy (established clinical patterns)

## Task 7: Getting In and Out of Bed

### Movement Sequence
- Typical: supine → sidelying → sitting → standing [33]
- Bed rise difficulty characterized by: increased UE use, discontinuity of trunk/leg motion, multiple shoulder/pelvic adjustments, multiple leg adjustments, poor vertical heel clearance [33]

### Muscles Driving Each Phase
- **Rolling (supine → sidelying):** SCM more enhanced on mobile side; PM larger activation on stable side; external oblique and rectus femoris contribute [34]. Hemiparetic patients: PM, EO, and RF on paretic side show reduced activation [34]
- **Supine → sitting:** Lateral trunk flexors and UE push critical; increased UE reliance with difficulty [33]
- **Sitting → standing:** Hip and knee extensors primary [16][35]

### Balance and Strength Demands
- BRD scale identifies five key difficulty items: UE pushes, discontinuity, multiple adjustments (shoulder/pelvic + leg), poor heel clearance [33]

### Evidence on Functional Decline
- Healthcare workers performing patient transfers experience high physical loads (nRMS 32.7, 95% CI: 28.9-36.6) — indicates substantial strength required [36]

### Clinical Populations
- Stroke, post-surgical, frail older adults, neuromuscular conditions

### Standardized Assessment Tools
- BRD scale validated [33]; FIM includes bed mobility

## Task 8: Sustained Overhead Work

### Shoulder Elevation ROM and Endurance
- At 80° arm elevation: deltoid force ~22% maximum, supraspinatus ~25% maximum, GH joint force ~43% body weight [37]
- At 80° elevation, endurance time ~5 minutes [37]

### EMG and Fatigue During Prolonged Overhead Work
- For postures maintained >1 hour: elevation angle should be <15° to prevent fatigue [37]
- MADC for overhead work varies: at 5 N load 0-40%; at 20 N load never exceeds 14% [38]
- Ergonomic "sweet spot" slightly above and forward of shoulder maximizes MADC [38]

### Cervical Spine Demands
- Activities requiring concurrent cervical and lumbar spine motion show greatest motion totals, typically >10°/sec rates of excursion [39]

### Shoulder Impingement Risk
- Limited evidence for association between arm elevation and shoulder disorders overall
- Moderate evidence for severe arm elevation with elbows above shoulder (>90°) [40]
- Studies with large effects (OR ≥2) report exposure-response relationships at >90° [40]

### Core and Lower Extremity Stabilization
- Overhead reaching uses 20-40% of maximum cervical ROM and 40-60% of maximum lumbar ROM [39]
