# OpenEvidence Response — Hand Intrinsic Muscle Movement Roles

Retrieved: 2026-03-29

---

## 1. Finger Adduction (MCP joint)

- Three palmar interossei adduct index, ring, and little fingers toward middle finger [1]
  - 1st PI → index finger adduction
  - 2nd PI → ring finger adduction  
  - 3rd PI → little finger adduction
  - No PI for middle finger (reference axis)
- Adductor pollicis does NOT contribute to finger adduction — solely thumb adduction [2]
- "Pollical palmar interosseous" (PPI) present in 91-93% of hands, acts on thumb [3-4]
- Stabilizers: FDP, FDS (MCP stability), extensor digitorum (maintaining extension) [5-6]

## 2. Thumb Adduction

- Adductor pollicis is primary mover, both heads contribute [2][7-8]
- Oblique head: significantly greater PCSA (p=0.008) — more force-generating capacity [7]
- Transverse head: smaller PCSA, greater fascicle length:muscle length ratio — more joint excursion [7]
- First palmar interosseous contributes in 91-93% of individuals [3-4]
- EMG: during isometric adduction, adductor pollicis shows linear EMG-force relationships [8]
- During pinch: adductor pollicis and FPL show nearly equal activation [8]

## 3. Finger DIP Flexion

- FDP is the sole muscle crossing DIP joint — only primary mover [9-11]
- In slope grip: FDP displays greater shear modulus early in contraction for DIP stabilization [10]
- Power grip: FDP is "workhorse" with peak stresses at DIP [9][12]
- Fox et al. (2019): FDP primary in both light and tight fist formation [12]
- Lumbricals and interossei do NOT directly act at DIP for flexion [5][13]

## 4. Finger DIP Extension

- DIP extension via lateral bands from lumbricals and interossei → terminal tendon → distal phalanx [5][14-15]
- Extensor digitorum CANNOT fully extend DIP alone — intrinsic contribution required [6][14]
- Murai et al. (2012): intrinsics and extrinsics contribute similarly to DIP extension force [14]
- Ulnar nerve palsy: DIP extension significantly impaired — classic "claw hand" [6][16-17]

## 5. Power Grip vs Precision Grip

- Power grip: greater muscle synchronization, higher extrinsic flexor activation [18-20]
- FDP provides main driving force; FDS contributes during tight fist [9][12]
- Intrinsics: lower relative contribution during power grip vs precision grip [20]
- Tip pinch: adductor pollicis and FPL nearly equal primary movers [8]
- Lateral (key) pinch: adductor pollicis and EPL primary [8]
- Unstable pinch: APB and EPL significantly more activated [21]
- Extrinsic thumb muscles significantly more active than intrinsics across all functional tasks [22]

## 6. Scapular Downward Rotation

- Rhomboids and levator scapulae are primary downward rotators [23-24]
- Pectoralis minor does NOT contribute to downward rotation — causes anterior tilt and IR [25-27]
- Rhomboid major: 25-45% MVIC during scapular exercises [23-24]
- LS/RM ratio approximately 0.85-0.99 during certain exercises [23]
- Functional importance: arm lowering from overhead, reaching behind back [23-24]

## 7. Upper Cervical Extension

- Suboccipital muscles (RCPMaj, RCPMin, OCS) specifically extend AO joint (~50% total cervical F/E) [32-34]
- RCPMaj and RCPMin: primary AO extensors; OCS also contributes [33]
- OCI primarily produces atlantoaxial rotation, not extension [33]
- Semispinalis capitis: significantly larger T2 shifts during CCE at C2-3 (p=0.03) and C5-6 (p=0.02) [35]
- Preferentially activated during upper cervical extension vs full cervical extension [35]
- AO joint: 13-21° extension; AA joint: 11-12° extension [34][38]
