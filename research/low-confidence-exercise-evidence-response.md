# OpenEvidence Response — Evidence for Common Rehabilitation Exercises

Retrieved: 2026-03-29

---

## Exercises With Moderate to Strong Evidence

### Copenhagen Adduction Exercise
**EMG Activation:** Peak adductor longus 108% MVIC (highest among hip adduction exercises); significantly higher than sidelying hip adduction (14% MVIC) [1]. Bilateral differences of 35-48% between legs [1].

**Dosing:** 8-week protocols with progressive loading. Training increased adductor muscle thickness 17-18% with significant flexibility improvements (+7%) [2]. Progressed by support location (knee, mid-leg, ankle); ankle support produces highest hip adductor moment (1.54 Nm/kg) [3].

**Clinical Effectiveness:** Recommended for groin injury prevention and adductor-related groin pain. Active supervised PT including adduction strengthening shows higher return-to-play rates than passive modalities [4-5].

### Nordic Hamstring Exercise
**EMG Activation:** Highest hamstring activation among eccentric exercises. Semitendinosus significantly higher than other exercises [6-7]. Peak forces highest in NHE though coinciding with relatively low muscle activation [8].

**Dosing:** 3-5 sets of 6-12 repetitions, 1-2 times/week. Greater fascicle lengthening vs RDL and single-leg deadlift [8].

**Clinical Effectiveness:** Most effective for BF short head and semitendinosus strength. May be superior for hamstring injury prevention [9-10].

**Progressions:** Varying lower leg support slope (0°, 20°, 40°) and hip flexion angles (0°, 25°, 50°) allows progression while maintaining torque demands [11].

### Clamshell Exercise
**EMG Activation:** Gluteus medius 32.6-54.4% MVIC [12-13]. Activation increases with hip flexion angle; 60° produces greatest gluteus medius activation [12]. Neutral pelvis significantly greater activation than reclined [12].

**Dosing:** fMRI: 40 reps produce 14.5% piriformis, 24.2% obturator internus activation increase [14]. Highest gluteus medius-to-TFL ratio (115) [15-16].

**Clinical Effectiveness:** Appropriate for Phase I-II hip rehabilitation (initial 4-8 weeks). Use caution with hip flexor tendinitis [17].

**Coaching Cues:** Maintain neutral pelvis, use 60° hip flexion for maximal glute med recruitment, avoid TFL compensation [12][16].

### Lateral Epicondylalgia Exercises
**Stretching:** Moderate evidence for stretching + strengthening over ultrasound and friction massage [18]. Static stretching 30-45 seconds, 3 reps before and after eccentric exercises [19].

**Strengthening:** Eccentric wrist extensor exercises with elbow extended, forearm pronated. 8-12 week eccentric loading program shows moderate evidence [18][20]. One RCT found no additional benefit from adding strengthening to education + stretching + activity modification at any timepoint up to 52 weeks [21].

### Thumb CMC Osteoarthritis Exercises
**Opposition:** Part of multimodal programs including opposition, paper tearing, line tracing, chopstick use, ball squeezing — 3x/week [22].

**Clinical Effectiveness:** Network meta-analysis: hand exercises reduce short-term pain (VAS -5.0 points, 95% CI -8.5 to -1.5) and produce clinically important grip strength improvement (21 pounds, 95% CI 11 to 31) vs control [23]. Multimodal treatment (exercises + splint + topical NSAID) produces small-to-medium function improvements at 6-12 weeks [22].

**Dosing:** Home program 3x/week for 6-8 weeks, adjusted at week 2 based on response [22][24].

### Schroth Exercises for Thoracic Kyphosis
**Dosing:** 2x/week for 8 weeks under physiotherapist supervision [25]. Programs ≤3 months show effectiveness [26].

**Clinical Effectiveness:** Schroth-based 3D exercises reduced thoracic kyphosis by -14.76° (large effect) vs -9.71° for postural corrective exercises [25]. 6-month spine-strengthening program reduced Cobb angle by -3.0° (95% CI -5.2 to -0.8) [27]. Low-to-moderate quality evidence supports ≤3 month structured programs [26].

### Hip Flexor and Hamstring Stretching
**Optimal Duration:** ACSM: 10-30 seconds general populations; older adults may benefit from 30-60 seconds [28]. 6-week daily wall stretches (30s to 2 min) showed no effect on ankle dorsiflexion ROM in one RCT [29].

**Clinical Effectiveness for LBP:** 8-week active hamstring stretching + core stabilization significantly reduced LBP during prolonged sitting [30]. Dynamic hip flexor stretching (5x/week, 8 weeks) in football players with LBP improved hip ROM (ES 3.01-3.06), reduced pain (ES 0.85) [31].

**Effect on Posture:** Single-session hip flexor stretching produced mean 1.2° reduction in anterior pelvic tilt but no change in lumbar lordosis; correlation between hip flexor length changes and pelvic tilt not meaningful [32].

### Overhead Press
**Seated vs Standing:** Standing dumbbell press produces ~8-15% higher anterior and medial deltoid activation vs seated [33]. Posterior deltoid ~24-25% higher standing vs seated [33]. Standing requires greater stability, ~7-10% lower 1RM [33].

**Barbell vs Dumbbell:** Dumbbell presses produce ~11-15% higher anterior deltoid activation than barbell [33]. Behind-neck press enhances medial and posterior deltoid vs front press [34].

### Resisted Internal Rotation
**EMG Activation:** Subscapularis 40-80% MVIC; highest in lift-off test position (44% active, 81.7% resisted at zero-position) [35-36].

**Exercise Selection:** Push-up plus and diagonal exercises produce greater subscapularis activation than traditional IR exercises [37]. Belly press test: subscapularis significantly higher than all other internal rotators [38].

**Dosing:** Rotator cuff protocols typically progress through 3 phases over 12-16 weeks [17].

### Triceps Extension — Overhead vs Other Positions
**EMG and Hypertrophy:** Overhead extension produces 1.5-fold greater long head hypertrophy (+28.5%) vs neutral position (+19.6%) over 12 weeks, despite lower absolute loads [39]. Greater muscle lengthening in overhead position [39].

**Dosing:** 70% 1RM, 10 reps/set, 5 sets/session, 2 sessions/week with progressive loading (+5% when completing without failure) [39].

### Calf Stretching
**Effectiveness:** Meta-analysis: calf stretching increases ankle dorsiflexion by 2-3° across all durations (≤15 min: 2.07°; 15-30 min: 3.03°; >30 min: 2.49°) [41]. 6 weeks of static stretching and foam rolling both produce ~18% dorsiflexion improvement; no synergistic combined effect [42].

**Weight-bearing vs Non-weight-bearing:** Both equally effective when performed 2x daily for 3 weeks (5 reps × 30 seconds) [43].

## Exercises With Limited or No Published Evidence

The following have minimal published EMG data or RCT-based dosing:
- Wrist radial/ulnar deviation — No specific EMG or dosing studies identified
- Seated hip internal rotation — Limited; pectineus moderate during rotational stabilization but not isolated IR [44]
- Forearm pronation/supination with hammer — General EMG data exists but not specific to hammer exercise [45-46]
- Sidelying hip adduction — Significantly lower activation (14% MVIC) than Copenhagen [1]
- Cervicothoracic self-mobilization — One RCT showing benefit when combined with exercise; optimal dosing unclear [47]
- Grip strengthening (squeeze ball) — Part of multimodal CMC OA programs but no isolated EMG data [22-23]
- Scapular rows, thoracic mobilization, diaphragmatic breathing, pelvic tilts, ankle band exercises, single-leg balance — Commonly prescribed but lack specific published EMG or standardized dosing from high-quality trials
